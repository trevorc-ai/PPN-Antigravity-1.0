The Problem: Patient Data as a Liability Asset

Most healthcare documentation systems were built for a different era, and a different regulatory context. Applying them to psychedelic-assisted therapy creates a liability exposure that practitioners and institutions have largely accepted as unavoidable, when it isn't.

The moment any documentation system stores a patient's name, date of birth, or address alongside a Schedule I substance protocol, the institution assumes significant legal and financial risk, regardless of how the session goes.

Average cost of a healthcare data breach⚠ $10.8 Million

Regulatory subpoena exposure (Schedule I protocols)⚠ Unlimited civil and criminal liability

These systems were not designed with this clinical context in mind. The gap between their identity-linked architecture and the regulatory landscape of this therapy class is a real liability risk, and most institutions carrying it are unaware of its full scope.

The PPN Architecture: Zero-Knowledge by Design

PPN Portal is not an EHR. It was built from the ground up as a clinical intelligence platform, and a core design principle was to store zero Protected Health Information. This was not a constraint to work around; it was the point.

Every patient record in the PPN registry is assigned a cryptographically generated, synthetic Subject ID at the moment of entry. No patient names, dates of birth, addresses, or identifying notes are ever entered, stored, or transmitted. This is not anonymization applied after the fact: it is a zero-knowledge design enforced from the start.

Three Pillars of Protection

01
Subpoena Immunity
If a regulatory body or law enforcement agency subpoenas the PPN database, the only data available for production is aggregate, anonymized metrics. Patient identity cannot be disclosed from a system that never recorded it.
02
Breach Liability: $0
Human error accounts for the majority of healthcare data breaches. By capturing no identifying information at the point of entry, PPN removes that exposure entirely. If the system were ever breached, there would be nothing of value to take.
03
Legal Cross-Site Benchmarking
PHI regulations prevent standard EHRs from enabling outcome comparisons across institutions. Because PPN stores no PHI, it is the only platform in this space that can legally benchmark outcomes across an entire clinical network.
