# **Drug Interaction Risks Between Psychedelic Substances and Concomitant Medications: A Forensic Clinical Research Report**

## **Section 1\. Executive Summary**

The rapid integration of psychedelic substances into clinical research and therapeutic practice necessitates rigorous, evidence-based screening for drug-drug interactions (DDIs). As the paradigm shifts from illicit recreational use to formalized clinical administration—evidenced by the FDA approval of esketamine and advancing phase 3 trials for MDMA and psilocybin 1—the patient populations seeking these treatments present increasingly complex psychiatric and systemic polypharmacy. This report provides an exhaustive, clinically conservative forensic analysis of interaction risks between classic and atypical psychedelics and common prescription, over-the-counter (OTC), and illicit substances. The analysis is built upon a standard of prioritizing the avoidance of false negatives, isolating established physiological dangers from theoretical risks, and unequivocally separating toxicity amplification from therapeutic efficacy blunting.

Analysis of pharmacodynamic (PD) and pharmacokinetic (PK) data reveals highly heterogeneous risk profiles across the psychedelic pharmacopeia. MDMA, an entactogen with amphetamine-like properties, and ayahuasca, a botanical preparation containing monoamine oxidase inhibitors (MAOIs), present the highest risks for life-threatening toxidromes. Specifically, the risk of severe serotonin syndrome and hypertensive crisis is profoundly elevated when these substances are co-administered with selective serotonin reuptake inhibitors (SSRIs), serotonin-norepinephrine reuptake inhibitors (SNRIs), and OTC antitussives such as dextromethorphan (DXM).3 Ibogaine presents a profound, well-documented risk of fatal ventricular arrhythmias due to hERG potassium channel blockade and subsequent QT prolongation, establishing an absolute contraindication with methadone, antiarrhythmics, and any agents inducing hypokalemia.7

Conversely, classic tryptamines (psilocybin, DMT) and lysergamides (LSD) demonstrate lower intrinsic physiological toxicity but present specific, severe risks in particular clinical subpopulations. Most notably, there is a striking incidence of seizures when classic psychedelics are co-administered with the mood stabilizer lithium.9 Furthermore, the concurrent use of classic psychedelics with serotonergic antidepressants primarily results in the attenuation (blunting) of psychedelic subjective effects rather than toxicity, a phenomenon driven by 5-HT2A receptor downregulation and competitive receptor occupancy resulting from chronic antidepressant administration.10 Atypical agents such as ketamine and esketamine present entirely distinct risk paradigms dominated by central nervous system (CNS) depression and respiratory risk when combined with opioids or benzodiazepines 11, alongside emerging clinical concerns regarding profound glycemic volatility when co-administered with diabetes medications such as insulin, metformin, or GLP-1 agonists.13

This report structures these findings to directly inform clinical safety screening logic, contraindication review, and the structural architecture of clinical decision-support systems.

## **Section 2\. High-Priority Red Flags**

The following combinations represent the most critical, life-threatening interactions identified in the literature. These pairs necessitate absolute contraindications within any screening platform, require rigorous clinical washout periods, and demand specialist medical oversight to prevent severe morbidity or mortality.

**1\. Ibogaine \+ QT-Prolonging Medications or Methadone (Fatal Arrhythmia Risk)**

* **Mechanism:** Ibogaine and its long-acting active metabolite, noribogaine, potently block hERG potassium channels. This action directly delays cardiac repolarization, manifesting as a prolonged QTc interval on an electrocardiogram.7 Co-administration with methadone (which carries its own intrinsic QT-prolonging properties) or other QT-prolonging agents exponentially increases the risk of Torsades de Pointes (TdP), leading to ventricular fibrillation and sudden cardiac death.7 Furthermore, genetic polymorphisms resulting in poor CYP2D6 metabolism can extend the half-life of ibogaine, exacerbating this cardiotoxicity.7  
* **Clinical Standard:** Absolute contraindication. Co-administration of any QT-prolonging agent, including certain antipsychotics, antiarrhythmics, and methadone, is strictly forbidden. Rigorous baseline ECG clearance, continuous cardiac monitoring, and aggressive management of electrolyte imbalances (specifically correcting hypokalemia and hypomagnesemia) are mandatory clinical prerequisites.7

**2\. Ayahuasca or Harmala Alkaloids \+ SSRIs, SNRIs, or Dextromethorphan (Serotonin Syndrome)**

* **Mechanism:** Ayahuasca is a traditional decoction that relies on the synergy of N,N-DMT and β-carboline alkaloids (harmine, harmaline, tetrahydroharmine). These β-carbolines act as reversible inhibitors of monoamine oxidase A (MAO-A).4 When MAO-A is inhibited, the normal enzymatic degradation of serotonin is halted. If Ayahuasca is combined with medications that inhibit serotonin reuptake (SSRIs, SNRIs, TCAs) or act as serotonin-releasing agents (such as the OTC cough suppressant dextromethorphan or the analgesic tramadol), the resulting synaptic accumulation of serotonin rapidly leads to severe, potentially fatal serotonin toxicity.4  
* **Clinical Standard:** Absolute contraindication. A stringent clinical washout period of 2 to 5 weeks is required prior to Ayahuasca ingestion, dictated heavily by the half-life of the specific antidepressant (e.g., a minimum of 5 weeks is clinically standard for fluoxetine due to its long-acting active metabolite, norfluoxetine).17

**3\. Classic Psychedelics (LSD, Psilocybin, Mescaline) \+ Lithium (Seizure Threshold Reduction)**

* **Mechanism:** While the precise neurochemical mechanism remains incompletely characterized, empirical pharmacovigilance data demonstrate a profound danger when combining the mood stabilizer lithium with classic psychedelics. Systematic reviews analyzing hundreds of case reports indicate that lithium dramatically lowers the seizure threshold in the presence of 5-HT2A agonists. In structured analyses, up to 47% of individuals combining lithium and classic psychedelics experienced seizures, with 39% requiring emergency medical intervention, and an additional 18% experiencing severe psychiatric distress ("bad trips").9  
* **Clinical Standard:** Absolute contraindication. Active lithium therapy must universally disqualify a patient from classic psychedelic administration. Notably, observational data indicate this risk is highly specific to lithium and does not appear to extend to lamotrigine, another common mood stabilizer, which observational cohorts have consumed alongside psychedelics without reported seizures.9

**4\. MDMA \+ CYP2D6 Inhibitors, MAOIs, or Serotonergic Agents (Toxicity Amplification and Toxidrome Risk)**

* **Mechanism:** MDMA acts primarily as a potent monoamine releasing agent, reversing the flow of serotonin, dopamine, and norepinephrine transporters. It is primarily metabolized by the hepatic enzyme CYP2D6. Crucially, MDMA acts as a mechanism-based auto-inhibitor of CYP2D6, resulting in non-linear pharmacokinetics.3 Co-administration with strong pharmaceutical CYP2D6 inhibitors (e.g., bupropion, fluoxetine, paroxetine) prevents the clearance of MDMA, drastically elevating plasma concentrations and magnifying cardiovascular load, hyperthermia, and neurotoxicity.3 Furthermore, combining the massive serotonin release of MDMA with MAOIs, DXM, or tramadol reliably triggers life-threatening serotonin syndrome.6  
* **Clinical Standard:** Absolute contraindication for MAOIs, DXM, and tramadol. Strong caution and mandatory contraindication review for CYP2D6 inhibitors and general SSRIs/SNRIs due to complex pharmacokinetic and pharmacodynamic overlaps.

**5\. Ketamine/Esketamine \+ Opioids, Benzodiazepines, or Alcohol (Synergistic CNS and Respiratory Depression)**

* **Mechanism:** Ketamine and its enantiomer esketamine are NMDA receptor antagonists that generally preserve respiratory drive when administered as monotherapy. However, co-administration with other central nervous system (CNS) depressants—most notably opioids, benzodiazepines, and alcohol—leads to a dangerous synergistic depression of the CNS. This combination can induce profound, unarousable sedation, loss of airway reflexes, coma, and potentially fatal respiratory arrest.11 Additionally, recent clinical and preclinical data suggest ketamine's rapid antidepressant effect relies, at least in part, on functional opioid receptor signaling, creating complex bidirectional interactions that complicate co-administration.22  
* **Clinical Standard:** Strong caution and highly restricted use. The FDA prescribing information and Risk Evaluation and Mitigation Strategy (REMS) protocols for esketamine explicitly mandate extreme caution, dose reduction, and rigorous clinical monitoring for respiratory depression when co-administered with any CNS depressants.11

## **Section 4\. Substance-by-Substance Review**

This section decomposes the unique pharmacokinetic and pharmacodynamic profiles of the requested substances, translating their metabolic pathways and receptor affinities into specific drug interaction phenotypes.

**Psilocybin (and Psilocin)** Psilocybin is a classic tryptamine pro-drug that undergoes rapid dephosphorylation to its active metabolite, psilocin. Unlike many psychiatric medications, psilocin metabolism relies primarily on UGT1A10, UGT1A9, MAO-A, and alkaline phosphatase, rendering it largely independent of the Cytochrome P450 (CYP) system.3 Consequently, pharmacokinetic (PK) interactions resulting in systemic toxicity are exceedingly rare. The primary clinical interaction concern is pharmacodynamic (PD). Concurrent use of SSRIs, SNRIs, or atypical antipsychotics leads to significant attenuation—commonly referred to as "blunting"—of the psychedelic effect.10 This blunting is driven by chronic antidepressant use causing downregulation of the target 5-HT2A receptors, as well as competitive binding at the receptor site.29 While historical literature theorized a risk of serotonin syndrome when combining psilocybin with SSRIs, contemporary scoping reviews and clinical trial data robustly demonstrate that this risk is vastly overstated; the combination is physically tolerable but therapeutically compromised.10 The most severe, clinically actionable safety contraindication for psilocybin is concurrent lithium use, which carries an unexplained but highly established risk of inducing severe seizures.9

**LSD (Lysergic Acid Diethylamide)** LSD is a potent ergoline derivative metabolized heavily by the liver via CYP1A2, CYP3A4, CYP2C9, CYP2C19, and CYP2D6.45 While it shares the primary 5-HT2A agonism profile of psilocybin, LSD exhibits much wider receptor affinities, engaging dopaminergic (D1, D2) and adrenergic receptors across the central and peripheral nervous systems.33 LSD acts as a peripheral 5-HT2A antagonist on platelets, leading to a theoretical, mechanism-based inhibition of platelet aggregation.33 While this implies a theoretical bleeding interaction with anticoagulants like warfarin or DOACs, clinically significant hemorrhage remains unsupported by human trials and is currently treated as a monitor-only risk.33 Acute cardiovascular load—manifesting as tachycardia and hypertension—is a standard physiological response, and very high recreational doses can provoke dangerous vasospasm.33 Like psilocybin, LSD's subjective effects are heavily blunted by SSRIs and abruptly halted by antipsychotics (which act as 5-HT2A and D2 antagonists).10 LSD also carries an absolute contraindication with lithium due to the aforementioned seizure risk.9

**MDMA (3,4-Methylenedioxymethamphetamine)** MDMA presents the most complex, multi-directional, and dangerous interaction profile among the substances reviewed. Functionally an entactogen with amphetamine-like sympathomimetic properties, MDMA induces massive vesicular release of serotonin, dopamine, and norepinephrine.3 It is primarily metabolized by CYP2D6, but crucially, MDMA acts as a mechanism-based auto-inhibitor of this very enzyme, rapidly creating non-linear pharmacokinetics where small dose increases lead to disproportionate spikes in blood plasma levels.3 Co-administration with pharmaceutical CYP2D6 inhibitors (e.g., fluoxetine, paroxetine, bupropion, duloxetine) drastically elevates MDMA plasma concentrations, magnifying its cardiovascular toxicity and neurotoxic potential.3 Pharmacodynamically, combining MDMA's massive serotonin release with MAOIs, the OTC antitussive dextromethorphan (DXM), or the atypical opioid tramadol reliably provokes life-threatening serotonin syndrome.5 Furthermore, co-administration with other stimulants (e.g., amphetamines, cocaine) produces additive cardiovascular toxicity, severely elevating the risk of myocardial infarction or stroke.46

**Ketamine and Esketamine** Operating as non-competitive NMDA receptor antagonists, ketamine and its S-enantiomer esketamine differ fundamentally from classic serotonergic psychedelics. They are primarily metabolized by CYP3A4 and CYP2B6.24 The FDA prescribing information for Spravato (esketamine) explicitly warns against combined use with any central nervous system (CNS) depressants—including opioids, benzodiazepines, and alcohol—due to a profound risk of synergistic sedation, loss of airway reflexes, and respiratory depression.11 Ketamine reliably induces transient spikes in blood pressure and heart rate; combining it with psychostimulants (e.g., methylphenidate, amphetamines) or MAOIs amplifies these hypertensive risks, requiring rigorous cardiovascular monitoring.11 Emerging clinical data also highlight complex interactions with diabetes medications (metformin, insulin, GLP-1 agonists), where ketamine-induced sympathetic activation alters hepatic gluconeogenesis and causes dangerous glycemic volatility, presenting as either hyper- or hypoglycemia.13

**Ibogaine** Ibogaine, an iboga alkaloid utilized primarily for opioid use disorder interruption, represents the highest acute medical danger of any reviewed substance. It is metabolized by CYP2D6 into noribogaine. Both the parent compound and the metabolite potently block hERG potassium channels in the myocardium, severely delaying repolarization and prolonging the QT interval on an ECG.7 This electrophysiological disturbance persists for days due to noribogaine's long half-life. Interactions with any other QT-prolonging drugs (e.g., haloperidol, certain macrolide antibiotics), hypokalemia-inducing drugs (e.g., loop diuretics), or methadone frequently result in fatal Torsades de Pointes (TdP) arrhythmias.7 CYP2D6 inhibitors also unpredictably modulate its cardiotoxicity.7 Ibogaine must never be administered without rigorous medical clearance, electrolyte paneling, and continuous ECG monitoring.

**DMT and 5-MeO-DMT** N,N-DMT and 5-MeO-DMT are extremely rapid-acting, short-duration tryptamines rapidly deactivated in the gut and liver by MAO-A.31 When administered via inhalation, insufflation, or intravenous routes, they bypass this first-pass metabolism.49 However, the concurrent use of pharmaceutical MAOIs or botanical MAOIs (such as harmala alkaloids) inhibits their breakdown, drastically prolonging systemic exposure and risking severe serotonin toxicity.15 Case reports of 5-MeO-DMT combined with *Peganum harmala* (Syrian rue) detail severe, life-threatening toxidromes characterized by extreme agitation, hyperthermia, tachycardia, and rhabdomyolysis.15 While clinical trials utilizing pure 5-MeO-DMT monotherapy report a favorable short-term safety profile 50, interactions with SSRIs and SNRIs warrant high clinical caution due to the potency of 5-MeO-DMT's serotonergic agonism.39

**Ayahuasca** Ayahuasca is a traditional botanical decoction uniquely engineered to circumvent human metabolism, combining N,N-DMT (typically from *Psychotria viridis*) with potent MAO-A inhibitors (from the *Banisteriopsis caapi* vine).15 Because it contains baseline MAOIs (harmine, harmaline, tetrahydroharmine), ayahuasca inherits all classical pharmaceutical MAOI contraindications.17 It is absolutely contraindicated with SSRIs, SNRIs, dextromethorphan (DXM), tramadol, prescription amphetamines, and OTC decongestants (pseudoephedrine) due to imminent, highly established risks of hypertensive crisis and fatal serotonin syndrome.4

**Mescaline** A naturally occurring phenethylamine found in peyote and San Pedro cacti, mescaline structurally resembles neurotransmitters like dopamine and epinephrine, resulting in baseline sympathomimetic effects (tachycardia, hypertension) alongside its 5-HT2A mediated psychedelic effects.38 While generally recognized as possessing a higher safety ratio than MDMA or ibogaine 51, interactions with prescription stimulants, antihypertensives, or illicit sympathomimetics (cocaine) complicate the patient's cardiovascular status.37 Mescaline's subjective effects are likely blunted by SSRIs and antipsychotics, mirroring the profile of classic tryptamines.45

## **Section 5\. Medication-Class Review**

To operationalize this data for screening logic, the interaction landscape must be viewed through the lens of the concomitant medication classes.

**SSRIs and SNRIs (e.g., Fluoxetine, Sertraline, Venlafaxine, Duloxetine)**

* *With MDMA, Ayahuasca, or 5-MeO-DMT:* Absolute contraindication. The combination of reuptake inhibition (from the antidepressant) and massive serotonin release or MAO inhibition (from the psychedelic) creates a perfect storm for fatal serotonin syndrome, characterized by hyperthermia, clonus, autonomic instability, and delirium.3  
* *With Psilocybin, LSD, or Mescaline:* Efficacy blunting. Chronic SSRI/SNRI use downregulates target 5-HT2A receptors, significantly dampening or aborting the psychedelic response.10 Contrary to historical belief, serotonin toxicity risk with this specific combination is negligible.10 Clinicians face a risk-benefit decision regarding whether to taper the antidepressant (risking psychiatric destabilization) or proceed with a potentially blunted therapy session.10 Note that fluoxetine requires a 5 to 6-week washout due to its exceptionally long half-life.17

**MAOIs (e.g., Phenelzine, Tranylcypromine, Selegiline)**

* *Class-wide rule:* Absolute contraindication across almost all psychedelics. With MDMA, DMT, 5-MeO-DMT, and SSRIs, MAOIs prevent the enzymatic clearance of massive serotonin loads, invariably leading to life-threatening serotonin toxicity.3 With ketamine and esketamine, MAOIs severely exacerbate sympathomimetic pressor responses, risking acute hypertensive crisis.11

**Mood Stabilizers (Lithium vs. Lamotrigine)**

* *Lithium:* Absolute contraindication with classic psychedelics (LSD, Psilocybin). The mechanistic synergy is poorly understood, but observational data is undeniable: combining lithium with psychedelics results in a massive increase in seizure risk and severe psychiatric distress.9  
* *Lamotrigine:* Observational data suggest it acts neutrally; it does not significantly alter the psychedelic experience, nor does it appear to increase seizure risk.9

**Antipsychotics (e.g., Quetiapine, Olanzapine, Aripiprazole, Haloperidol)**

* Atypical antipsychotics primarily function as 5-HT2A and D2 antagonists.54 As such, they aggressively blunt or entirely terminate the hallucinogenic and emotional effects of classic psychedelics. They are frequently utilized in emergency or underground settings as pharmacological "trip killers" to abort challenging experiences.26 The primary clinical concerns are non-therapeutic over-sedation, hypotension, and the unintended pharmacological termination of therapeutic psychedelic processing.26 Typical antipsychotics like haloperidol also carry QT-prolonging risks, severely contraindicating them with ibogaine.7

**Benzodiazepines (e.g., Diazepam, Alprazolam, Clonazepam)**

* *With Classic Psychedelics:* Benzodiazepines act as GABA-A positive allosteric modulators. They mildly attenuate the cognitive and visual effects of classic psychedelics and are the standard-of-care rescue medications for managing acute, treatment-resistant anxiety or panic during a psychedelic session.3  
* *With Ketamine/Esketamine:* Strong caution. Combining benzodiazepines with ketamine creates a high risk of synergistic CNS sedation, respiratory depression, and prolonged recovery times.11

**Stimulants (e.g., Amphetamines, Methylphenidate, Cocaine)**

* *With MDMA or Mescaline:* Synergistic neurotoxicity and cardiovascular overload. The combination provokes severe hypertension, tachycardia, and hyperthermia, significantly increasing the risk of acute cardiovascular events.46  
* *With Ketamine/Esketamine:* Exacerbation of blood pressure spikes. The FDA label for esketamine mandates close blood pressure monitoring when co-administered with psychostimulants.11

**Opioids (including Methadone, Tramadol, and Buprenorphine)**

* *Methadone:* Absolute contraindication with Ibogaine due to compounded hERG blockade, severe QT prolongation, and lethal arrhythmia risk.7  
* *Tramadol:* Absolute contraindication with MDMA and Ayahuasca. Tramadol is an atypical opioid that also functions as a serotonin reuptake inhibitor, bringing a severe risk of serotonin syndrome and seizures when combined with serotonergic psychedelics.16  
* *All Opioids \+ Ketamine:* High risk of profound CNS and respiratory depression.12 Furthermore, recent mechanistic studies suggest ketamine's antidepressant efficacy may require active opioid receptors, creating a complex clinical paradigm regarding concurrent opioid therapy.22

**Antihypertensives and Beta Blockers (e.g., Propranolol, Lisinopril)**

* The use of beta-blockers (e.g., propranolol) can mask MDMA-induced tachycardia but consistently fails to prevent MDMA-induced hypertension. Mechanistically, blocking beta-2 vasodilation while leaving MDMA-induced alpha-adrenergic stimulation unopposed may hypothetically worsen the pressor response.34 Blood pressure medications may experience unpredictable dynamics during ketamine or ayahuasca administration due to acute, transient sympathetic spikes.4

**OTC Cold, Flu, and Allergy Medications**

* *Dextromethorphan (DXM):* A common cough suppressant that acts as both a serotonin reuptake inhibitor and a releasing agent. It is an absolute contraindication with MDMA and Ayahuasca due to rapid, severe serotonin syndrome.6  
* *Pseudoephedrine/Phenylephrine:* OTC sympathomimetic decongestants that dramatically increase blood pressure and heart rate when combined with MDMA, Mescaline, or Ketamine.42

**Diabetes Medications (Insulin, Metformin, GLP-1 Agonists)**

* *With Ketamine:* Ketamine initiates a strong sympathetic outflow, causing epinephrine release that alters hepatic gluconeogenesis and suppresses endogenous insulin secretion.13 This causes dangerous glycemic volatility—presenting as acute hyperglycemia followed by potential delayed hypoglycemia. This requires intensive bedside blood glucose monitoring, especially in T1DM patients or those on intensive insulin, metformin, or GLP-1 therapy.13

**Anticoagulants and Antiplatelets (e.g., Warfarin, Aspirin)**

* LSD acts as a peripheral 5-HT2A antagonist, which theoretically inhibits platelet aggregation and vascular smooth muscle proliferation.33 While the mechanistic and *in vitro* data are sound, translation to clinical danger (e.g., spontaneous hemorrhage) when combined with warfarin or DOACs is weakly supported and remains a theoretical, monitor-only risk.33

## **Section 6\. Conflicts, Ambiguities, and Evidence Gaps**

A strict forensic review of the psychopharmacological literature reveals several areas where prevailing clinical dogmas conflict with emerging empirical evidence. A robust screening platform must account for these nuances.

1. **Overstated Serotonin Syndrome Risk (Classic Psychedelics \+ SSRIs):** Early literature and underground lore aggressively warned of life-threatening serotonin syndrome when combining classic tryptamines (psilocybin, LSD) with SSRIs. However, contemporary scoping reviews and phase 2/3 clinical trial data (from sponsors like COMPASS Pathways and MAPS) demonstrate that this combination primarily leads to *efficacy blunting* via receptor downregulation, not physical toxicity.10 True, life-threatening serotonin toxicity is a genuine threat primarily reserved for MDMA, 5-MeO-DMT, and Ayahuasca.5  
2. **Valvulopathy and Microdosing Uncertainty:** There is a highly plausible theoretical risk that chronic, repeated use of LSD or psilocybin (e.g., via "microdosing" regimens) activates 5-HT2B receptors, initiating profibrotic signaling cascades that lead to valvular heart disease.33 This hypothesis is based on structural similarities to ergot derivatives (like fenfluramine or methysergide), which caused famous epidemics of cardiac fibrosis. However, robust, long-term human echocardiogram data confirming this risk in psychedelic users is currently absent.33  
3. **Ketamine Opioid-Mechanism Ambiguity:** Recent neuroimaging and pharmacological blockade studies suggest that ketamine's rapid antidepressant effect requires functional, unblocked opioid receptors.22 This creates a therapeutic dilemma: co-administering naltrexone (an opioid antagonist) might neutralize ketamine's psychiatric efficacy, while co-administering agonist opioids risks profound respiratory depression.12  
4. **Brand-Name vs. Ingredient Confusion (The LASA Problem):** Digital interaction checkers frequently fail because they map at the therapeutic class level rather than the specific ingredient level.59 A patient reporting the use of "Advil Cold and Sinus" is unknowingly exposed to pseudoephedrine (a severe hypertension risk with MDMA or Ayahuasca), while standard "Advil" (ibuprofen) has no meaningful PD overlap and is safe.18 Screening tools must map to granular RxNorm or SNOMED ingredient codes, not display names.

## **Section 7\. Screening Logic Recommendations**

For a clinical decision-support platform, the nuanced evidence described above must be systematically translated into actionable, deterministic "rule buckets." These buckets must prioritize safety (false negative avoidance) over therapeutic access, guiding the clinician via clear severity flags.

The logic architecture categorizes interactions into a severity continuum. The overarching goal is to prevent the clinician from proceeding when a physiological threat exists, while providing rich contextual data when a therapeutic threat (blunting) exists.

* **Absolute Contraindication (Hard Stop \- Red):** Combinations posing an imminent, documented risk of death, seizure, or severe medical emergency. Generates a hard block in screening software.  
  * *Rules:* Ibogaine \+ QT prolongers/Opioids; Ayahuasca/MAOIs \+ SSRIs/SNRIs/DXM/Tramadol; Classic Psychedelics \+ Lithium; MDMA \+ MAOIs/DXM/Tramadol.  
* **Strong Caution (Requires Washout or Exemption \- Orange):** Combinations with high toxicity potential that may be managed with rigorous, documented clinical protocols, dose adjustments, or strict washout periods.  
  * *Rules:* Ketamine \+ Benzodiazepines/Opioids; MDMA \+ CYP2D6 inhibitors (e.g., bupropion, fluoxetine).  
* **Needs Clinician Review (Complex Status \- Yellow):** Physiologically variable interactions requiring baseline medical assessment, specialist consultation, or intra-session vitals monitoring.  
  * *Rules:* Ketamine \+ Insulin/GLP-1s/Metformin; MDMA \+ Antihypertensives; Ketamine \+ Thyroid medication.  
* **Possible Efficacy Blunting (Therapeutic Choice \- Yellow/Gray):** No immediate physical danger or toxicity, but the intended therapeutic psychological effect will be severely compromised. Forces a risk-benefit analysis on tapering.  
  * *Rules:* Psilocybin/LSD \+ SSRIs/SNRIs; Psychedelics \+ Atypical Antipsychotics.  
* **Monitor Only (Minor \- Gray):** Interactions that are largely theoretical, mechanistic, or produce mild, manageable side effects.  
  * *Rules:* LSD \+ Warfarin/Anticoagulants; Psilocybin \+ NSAIDs.  
* **Insufficient Evidence:** Compounds lacking basic pharmacokinetic or pharmacodynamic data in human cohorts, barring automated classification.

## **Section 8\. Structured Data Schema Recommendation**

To architect an interaction checker that captures the forensic nuance of this report, the underlying database must move beyond simplistic, binary "Drug A \+ Drug B \= Bad" logic. The schema must meticulously capture directionality, severity, confidence, and mechanism, allowing the frontend to generate precise, medically accurate practitioner alerts.

The following relational table design is recommended for storing and querying this interaction intelligence:

**Table: clinical\_interaction\_rules**

* interaction\_id (UUID, Primary Key) \- *Unique identifier for the interaction rule.*  
* psychedelic\_substance (String, e.g., "MDMA", "Ibogaine") \- *The primary investigational agent.*  
* medication\_class (String, e.g., "SNRI", "Anticoagulant") \- *Allows for broad class-based flagging.*  
* medication\_ingredient (String, e.g., "Venlafaxine", "Lithium") \- *Crucial for allowing specific exceptions to class rules (e.g., tramadol acts differently than morphine).*  
* external\_code\_system (String, e.g., "RxNorm") \- *Standardizes terminology for interoperability.*  
* external\_code (String, e.g., "32968") \- *The specific identifier in the external system.*  
* mechanism (String, e.g., "CYP2D6 competitive auto-inhibition", "hERG channel blockade") \- *The pharmacological rationale.*

* severity (Enum: Contraindicated, Major, Moderate, Mild, Negligible) \- *Drives UI alerts.*  
* confidence (Enum: High, Moderate, Low) \- *Reflects the evidence quality.*  
* evidence\_level (Enum: FDA Label, Consensus Guideline, Clinical Trial, Observational, Case Report, Mechanistic Theory) \- *The source hierarchy tier.*  
* screening\_note (Text) \- *Practitioner-facing instruction (e.g., "Do not proceed without ECG").*  
* washout\_note (Text) \- *Specific chronological guidance (e.g., "Minimum 5 half-lives required, approximately 5 weeks for fluoxetine").*  
* citation\_text (Text) \- *The academic or regulatory citation.*  
* citation\_url\_or\_identifier (Text) \- *DOI or URL for verification.*  
* review\_status (Enum: Active, Under Review, Deprecated) \- *For QA maintenance.*  
* last\_reviewed\_at (Timestamp) \- *Ensures data currency.*

## **Section 10\. Final Recommendation**

**What to Automate:** A production-grade clinical interaction checker should strictly automate absolute contraindications based on well-established pharmacokinetic and pharmacodynamic mechanisms. Combinations that pose an immediate risk to life (e.g., Ayahuasca \+ MAOIs, Ibogaine \+ QT prolongers, MDMA \+ CYP2D6 inhibitors, Classic Psychedelics \+ Lithium) must trigger automated, hard-stop warnings. Furthermore, the automation layer must handle ingredient-level mapping to prevent potentially fatal brand-name confusion (e.g., correctly parsing "NyQuil" to identify DXM, thereby flagging a severe MDMA contraindication).

**What Not to Automate:** The software platform should *not* automate therapeutic tapering schedules or attempt to dictate psychiatric washout decisions. Distinguishing between a dangerous physical toxidrome (e.g., MDMA \+ SSRI) and simple therapeutic efficacy blunting (e.g., Psilocybin \+ SSRI) requires nuanced clinical judgment regarding the individual patient's baseline psychiatric stability and suicide risk. The checker's role is to surface the forensic evidence clearly (e.g., displaying the alert: "Combination risks therapeutic blunting via receptor downregulation, not serotonin syndrome") and defer the complex risk-benefit analysis of medication cessation entirely to the attending prescribing clinician.
